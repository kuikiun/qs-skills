# ACMM Vol 3 — Life Cycle Costing

LCC = assessment of design alternatives considering **all the significant costs of ownership** — initial capital plus running costs over the building's economic/effective life.

## §1 Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Life Cycle Costing** | The methodology — assessment of design alternatives considering all costs of ownership. Ultimate objective: optimum design decisions. Can apply to total building or any component |
| **Life Cycle Cost** | Total cost of providing, owning, operating and maintaining a building or component over its useful life. Expressed in equivalent monetary terms; divided into Capital Cost + Cost-In-Use |
| **Cost-In-Use** | Cost of ownership — operating, maintenance, cleaning, alterations, replacement, support. EXCLUDES initial capital and demolition/disposal |

## §2 Background

- Capital costs typically only **30–40%** of total ownership cost
- Cleaning costs sometimes exceed initial installation cost (e.g. floor coverings)
- Operating and maintenance costs over building life can outweigh initial costs
- Owner-occupiers benefit most; developers (selling/leasing) benefit less
- Public sector for community needs; private sector influenced by ROI

## §3 Concept — what LCC does and doesn't do

| LCC does | LCC does not |
|---|---|
| Provide formal disciplined logical methodology | Compare client options of "build / renovate / refurbish / lease / do nothing" — these are pre-design |
| Compare design alternatives quantitatively | Take into account non-quantifiable aspects (aesthetics, function, environmental effect) |
| Build a cost data bank for future LCC | Replace cost-benefit analysis (which has financial + economic, broader view) |
| Apply to total building or any component (e.g. air-conditioning, roof, gutter) | |

### LCC general process
(a) Choose design alternatives for definitive cost studies  
(b) Identify common items/elements; remove from study (same for all options); some decided by other factors  
(c) Establish capital cost and Cost-In-Use for each alternative  
(d) Convert cash flow to common time basis using discounting  
(e) Sensitivity analysis (variations in important figures)  
(f) Choose final design — also considering function, aesthetics, quality

**Earlier is better.** The later LCC is introduced, the lower the potential for decision-making cost savings, and the more expensive any proposed changes.

## §4 Evaluation Period / Life Span

The most critical aspect — assessment of economic or effective life.

| Type of obsolescence | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Economic | When more economical to replace than maintain |
| Functional | When component will cease to function for its original purpose |
| Physical | When physical collapse imminent |
| Technological | When technical advances demand replacement |
| Social and Legal | When human desires or legislation dictate replacement for non-economic reasons |

In most cases **economic life < physical life** (a commodity becomes useless before it falls over).

## §5 LCC Components

### 5.1 Capital costs
Land, surveys, demolition, relocation, legal fees; design (consultants + in-house, special studies); construction (labour, material, equipment, admin, overheads, overtime, profit, insurances, permits, fees); commissioning and testing. **Usually non-recurring.**

### 5.2 Operating costs
Fuel, energy sources, charges, all labour for effective functioning incl. security. **Most significant operating cost: energy.**

### 5.3 Maintenance costs
Cost of retaining or restoring asset to agreed condition; includes custodian care + repair.

**Three activities NOT classified as maintenance:**
1. Complete replacement (small components yes, complete plant items at end of life no)
2. Rehabilitation to new standard or new use
3. Daily cleaning (preventative aim is secondary; usually different operatives; significant separate cost)

**Two types:**
- **Type A — Corrective Maintenance** — work initiated by report of failure outside preventative maintenance inspection
- **Type B — Programmed Maintenance** — work programmed and budgeted for; statutory inspections, regular maintenance, testing, monitoring, measurement, adjustment/replacement

### 5.4 Cleaning costs
Production rates 200 m²/hr to 700 m²/hr (mean ~450 m²/hr). For carpeted office: 500 m²/hr. Vinyl 100–125 m²/hr less.

Part-time evening labour more efficient than full-time (despite penalty rates) due to productivity.

Cost components: labour (wages, penalty/relief/leave loadings, super, workers' comp, public risk insurance), capital expenditure on machinery/vehicles, expendable materials/clothing, accommodation overheads, administrative overheads.

### 5.5 Alteration and replacement costs
Alteration: associated with future changes in function. Replacement: cost of replacing capital equipment/materials that can't economically survive expected facility life.

### 5.6 Support costs
Insurances, rates, management expenses.

### 5.7 Total costs
Pie chart of typical office building over 40-year life span. Cleaning, maintenance, operating components similar order for office buildings.

### 5.8 Demolition and disposal costs
Not part of Cost-In-Use. Alternatively: residual values applied at end of evaluation period as a "negative cost" — likely saleable / market value of facility or component, or scrap value for demolition.

### 5.9 Cost data
LCC requires reliable cost data in standard format — to provide collection framework, allow project-stage data to relate uniformly, focus on high-cost areas, avoid communication blocks, provide checklists.

## §6 Application to Design/Documentation Process

Apply LCC across the four ACMM stages:

| Stage | What |
|---|---|
| **Brief stage (feasibility/pre-design)** | Position on site, general form. Approximate Costs-In-Use studies for several different general concepts. Considerations: alternative locations, orientation, building shape/volume, general "shell" design, engineering systems and energy sources, suitability for purpose, aesthetics |
| **Outline proposals stage (schematic)** | Compare relative costs of components: open plan vs fixed partitioning; fenestration vs HVAC + lighting; HVAC capital options vs Cost-In-Use budgets |
| **Sketch design stage** | Choice of individual components; Cost-In-Use budgets confirmed |
| **Documentation stage** | Restricted to outstanding design work; usually all design decisions previously made. Detailed Cost-In-Use budgets prepared for building management |

## §7 Discounting

The principle: capital today > capital next year (today's earns return in interim). Discounting reduces future sums to equivalent today amount (Net Present Cost).

### Discount Rate
Rate reflecting time value of money used to convert benefits/costs at different times to equivalent at common point in time. Expressed as % per annum.

**Recommended REAL Discount Rate** (without inflation): approximately **10% for public sector projects**.

**Sensitivity Analysis** with REAL Discount Rates 7%–13% should be applied to check for significant differences in economic ranking.

Sample table at 10% cost of capital:
- $10 received 1 year hence = $9.09 now
- $10 received 2 years hence = $8.26 now
- $10 received 3 years hence = $7.51 now

## §8 Techniques in the Use of Discounting

### 8.1 Net Present Cost (NPC)
Discounted present cost of all expenses (incl. capital outlay) over the evaluation period.

### 8.2 Annual Equivalent Cost (AEC)
Series of uniform annual amounts that, when discounted and summed, equal the NPC.

### When to use which

| Use | Method |
|---|---|
| Alternatives have **same** expected lives | NPC |
| Alternatives have **different** expected lives | AEC (NPC method limited because calculations relate to different time periods; dividing NPC by life is wrong because it ignores money's time value) |

Once cash flows become more complex, NPC simpler than AEC. AEC's main benefit: variable economic lives with annual costs occurring; basic assumption is that annual equivalent charge for short-life option re-occurs at same rate for full evaluation period and replacement cost = initial.

### 8.3 Application
Develop alternative means in sufficient detail for reasonable cost estimates. Estimate capital, operating, maintenance + expected life and residual value for each year of each alternative. Discount estimated costs by Present Value Factor → arrive at NPC or AEC → identify lowest figure.

Discount tables — Present Value Factors — in Vol 3 Appendices 2, 3, 4.

## §9 Data

### Data sources
- **Historical data** — indicates where Cost-In-Use is traditionally high (finishes, mech/electrical services, maintenance). Use professional judgment.
- **Modelling techniques** — calculate operating costs from first principles; mechanical equipment maintenance/replacement, cleaning, heat load, energy.
- **Specialist manufacturers** — operating life, maintenance, cleaning, energy use of components.
- **Property Council of Australia (PCA)** — useful data for commercial office buildings.

### Collection of data — system addresses
- Cost-generating activities over facility life (initial procurement, support functions like maintenance, cleaning)
- Facility components (sub-structure, windows, chiller)
- Functional spaces
- Resources (materials, direct labour)
- Materials and construction characteristics
- Type of use (hospital, ward wing, boiler house)
- Standard of quality (prestige / average / utilitarian — three-level assessment)
- Other factors (location, size, age, user expectations, intensity of use)

### Activities — three sub-divisions
Initial procurement, Costs-In-Use, disposal. Support activities → maintenance, operation, reconstruction, other.

### Facility components — use AIQS standard elements/sub-elements
Vol 1 Appendix A + Vol 2 detailed definitions.

### Functional spaces
Per Vol 1 Appendix A part 7. Specific functional spaces may sub-divide elements (e.g. Floors → office / lecture theatre / laboratory / car parking).

## §10 Worked Example — Floor Finishes (key example)

Aim: compare 11 floor finishes in office foyer/entrance area (heavily trafficable, daily cleaning) over 30-year evaluation period using NPC and AEC methods.

### Floor finishes and life spans
| Finish | Life (years) |
|---|---|
| Nairn Armourflex 2.5mm linoleum sheet | 20 |
| Invicta Crusader II heavy carpet | 8 |
| Invicta Tutor II heavy carpet | 7 |
| Invicta Saracen Antron III nylon carpet | 6 |
| PGH Eureka Sovereign quarry tile | 30 |
| Prime Duraker ceramic floor tile | 40 |
| GH Johnston River hardwood parquet | 40 |
| GH Cypress Pine parquet | 25 |
| Hardwood T&G strip flooring | 30 |
| Cypress Pine T&G strip flooring | 30 |
| Terrazzo Fritztile C700 | 20 |

Costs evaluated: installation, maintenance, cleaning, replacement, residual value (negative cost in NPC).

### Cleaning cost build-up example (carpet)
Vacuum 900 m²/hr at $22/hr; vacuum + cleaning accessories say $1.50 → $23.50 / 900 = 2.6 c/m²/day. Daily × 247 working days/year = $6.45/m²/yr. With other carpet activities → $10/m²/yr.

### Result (10% discount, ascending NPC over 30 years)
| Order | Finish | NPC ($/m²) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Cypress Pine T&G | 60.33 |
| 2 | Hardwood T&G | 64.32 |
| 3 | Cypress Pine Parquetry | 102.85 |
| 4 | Johnson River Hardwood Parquetry | 108.95 |
| 5 | Invicta Saracen | 114.92 |
| 6 | Invicta Crusader | 123.47 |
| 7 | Invicta Tutor | 124.87 |
| 8 | Nairn Armourflex | 129.74 |
| 9 | Duraker Ceramic | 132.76 |
| 10 | PGH Eureka Quarry | 139.89 |
| 11 | Terrazzo Fritztile | 152.70 |

### Conclusions
- **Timber floors** (T&G strip / parquetry) most economical — sweep only
- **Carpet** next — daily vacuum
- **Hard finishes** (linoleum, ceramics, quarry tiles) most expensive — sweep + polish/buff or wet clean (two activities)
- **High initial cost + long life ≠ economic** (Terrazzo example)
- **Low initial cost + short life ≠ expensive** (Saracen carpet example)
- Selection for heavy traffic: type and method of cleaning need careful attention from economic viewpoint
- Variable discount rates (7%, 13%) don't significantly change order; cleaning costs more dominant at lower discount rate
- Other factors (insulation, noise, slip safety, ease of cleaning, staining, aesthetics) may dominate financial comparison

## §11 Building Engineering Services LCC

### 11.1 Issues to address
- Data collection — reliable feedback of Cost-In-Use
- Economic Life of Services — system + equipment choice
- Critical factors affecting Cost-In-Use: Energy
- Annual energy consumption — standardised collection
- Energy forecasting and recording — designers ensure services designed to budgets
- Critical factors affecting Cost-In-Use: Maintenance
- Maintenance forecasting and recording — like energy budgets

### 11.2 Data collection — link
The vital link is **factual data on Costs-In-Use of existing buildings**.

Information loop: Operating authority → Designer feedback. Designers obtain feedback from operators → provide operating budgets as part of design.

### 11.5 Effect of cost movement
Energy use tracked in **energy units** (not $) — eliminates cost-increase noise from data. Apply current energy rates to convert. For maintenance use a "maintenance cost index" based on suitable labour index. Operating wages updated using labour index.

## §12 Economic Life of Building Engineering Services

For mathematical calculation, anticipate number of replacements within building life. Consider **systems** rather than components (e.g. lighting systems ~20 yrs, but not lamps — consider those as cost-benefit selection).

Various terms with different shades of meaning: useful life / effective life / economic life / technological life / replacement life / physical life.

Pump example:
- Various sources: 15 / 25 / 20–25 / 20 years
- For 80-year building life: 4.3 / 2.2–3 / 3 / 3 replacements
- Use consistent values, adjust with experience
- Probably more error in future replacement unit cost than number of replacements

## §13 Critical Factors Affecting Cost-In-Use — Energy

### 13.1 Sydney office building energy targets

| Item | Energy intensity | MJ/m²/yr (2500 hrs/yr) |
|---|---|---|
| Lighting | 20 W/m² | 180 |
| Power | 5 W/m² | 45 |
| Lifts | 5–6 W/m² | 50 |
| Air-conditioning | 100 W/m² grand total heat | 200 + 75 (DHW) |

**Achievable target (current): 500 MJ/m²/yr** Net Rentable Area.

**Ambitious targets** for new air-conditioned office buildings (coastal NSW, 2500 hrs/yr):
- Lighting (400 lux at 14 W/m² max): **140 MJ/m²/yr**
- Power on tenant floor (5 W/m²): **30 MJ/m²/yr**
- Air-conditioning + ventilation: **160 MJ/m²/yr**
- Purchased heating: **20 MJ/m²/yr**
- Domestic hot water: **10 MJ/m²/yr**
- Lifts: **40 MJ/m²/yr**
- **Total target: 400 MJ/m²/yr**

### 13.3 Measures to control energy usage

1. **Control absolute value of load to be met** — minimise heat gains/losses; orientation, insulation, shade coefficients, lighting levels, peak ventilation rates, air-tightness, year-round effect
2. **Control efficiency of energy conservation** — motors, fans, pumps, compressors; total system efficiency; component efficiencies (DX refrigeration, chilled water, electric resistance, boilers); multiple plant sized to match part-load
3. **Minimising mass of fluid being circulated and part-load performance** — design to realistic minimums; reduce fan/pump energy at part loads; consider direct electric element heating
4. **Heating and cooling cycles** — energy is well-engineered cycles is minimal compared to fans/pumps; if cycles get out of control, can swamp other factors. Bad design / commissioning / maintenance allows simultaneous heating and cooling
5. **Use of ambient conditions** — fresh air cycles, cold flushing before occupation, natural light
6. **Occupancy patterns and running hours** — match system to occupancy; turn off plant in non-occupied areas; limit running hours
7. **Choice of control system** — reliable; doing what it's meant to do; always

### 13.4 Critical detail
- **Fans laws:** Quantity ∝ speed; Head ∝ speed²; Power ∝ speed³
- Designer safety factor + Contractor minimum + Supplier minimum = fans/pumps usually arrive 15–20% **oversized** → significant running cost penalty
- Energy is wasted in very large quantities if heating + cooling allowed simultaneously, or fresh air cycles not properly controlled

### 13.5 Priority of critical factors

**Mechanical:**
1. Control of simultaneous heating and cooling and dampers for outside air cycles
2. Design and installation of control system in general
3. Limitation of absolute value of load
4. Control of part-load performance
5. Match system to occupancy pattern

**Electrical:** Lighting design, control and operation (significant cost element).

## §14 Annual Energy Consumption Form

Used at design / construction / operating stages, progressively updated:

| Column | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Item | Energy-consuming device by name |
| kW Rated | Nominal size |
| kW Running | Anticipated kW drawn (verified through construction stages) |
| Factor | Diversity factor (time, equivalent time at full load, or combination) |
| Nominal Hours | Hours of operation per year |
| kWH per Annum | kW Running × Factor × Nominal Hours |
| MJ/m²/Annum | (kWH/Annum × 3.6 / NRA) × (2500 / Operating Hours) |

NRA = Net Rentable Area.

Energy can be reduced by: (a) eliminating need for the item; (b) reducing running hours; (c) reducing kW running.

## When to apply this skill

When: comparing design alternatives by total cost of ownership rather than capital cost only; assessing floor finishes / lift contracts / mechanical plant / HVAC options on a Life Cycle Cost basis; selecting between high-capital-low-running vs low-capital-high-running options; setting energy and maintenance targets for a new building; reviewing a building's actual Cost-In-Use against forecasts; preparing an Annual Energy Consumption form; advising on replacement cycles for engineering services; advising on the optimum economic life or evaluation period for an LCC study; calculating NPC or AEC; running sensitivity analysis on discount rates.
