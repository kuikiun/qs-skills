Procedures for managing cost from contract award through final account. Aim: contain expenditure within pre-determined limits.

## §3.2 Documentation — what each form does

| Form | Description | Cost-management effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Specification + Drawings** | Specification = written description of materials and workmanship; drawings = pictorial description | Universally required (except maintenance/alterations where spec alone may suit) |
| **Bills of Quantities (BOQ)** | Produced independently, issued to all tenderers | Uniform basis for tender prep and adjudication; close scrutiny in BOQ production catches errors before tender; contract cost-management document; data source for cost analysis |
| **Schedule of Rates** | Rates for items, may or may not contain quantities | Payment per measured quantity; usually for minor/uncomplicated projects or where quantities can't be set |
| **Quantities Assessed by Tenderers** | No quantities provided | Tenderers compete on rates; quantities only for contractor's purposes; no feedback of cost info |

**Specified Bills of Quantities** integrate description and quantity into one document — avoids duplication.

**Provisional Quantities** — given when precise quantities can't be established at tender; replaced ASAP with actuals.

## §3.3 Tendering — four procedures

| Method | When to use | Risks/benefits |
|---|---|---|
| **Open** | Public bodies, widest market coverage | Risk of unacceptable Tenderer; high doc volume; need to handle low Tenderers carefully |
| **Selective** | Pre-categorised tenderers | Equal competition; reduced doc volume; consistent number of tenders; risk of "cover prices" |
| **Negotiation** | Single Contractor of proven ability | Reduced competition → higher price; minimum doc; suitable for staged/concurrent work |
| **Registration & Selection** | Combines open + selective | Public advertisement; only genuine offers; lowest tender normally accepted |

### Selective tendering — categorisation criteria
(a) financial status; (b) technical ability; (c) capacity for cost ranges; (d) type/quantity/extent/quality of work; (e) timing/manner of completion; (f) reputation/references.

### Tender appraisal
Tender documents should ensure:
- Construction times are **given**, not nominated by Tenderers
- **Conforming** tenders required; alternatives restricted to defined areas
- **Priced BOQs** required before acceptance for technical and mathematical check

**Technical check** — every item priced separately; pricing consistent and reasonable; cost of early trades not excessive; rates against Provisional Quantities suitable; Preliminaries priced separately (not loaded into trade rates); circulars/addenda accounted for; PSs and PCs correctly included; no text alterations.

**Mathematical check** — extensions correct; page totals carried correctly; summary mathematically correct, equals tender sum.

Compensating adjustments wherever possible **restricted to individual trades**, maintaining trade totals.

## §3.4 Specialists — three engagement methods

| Method | Description | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sub-contracts** | Contractor selects own sub-contractors (with Principal approval) | Better overall control; lower admin cost for Principal; single contract responsibility | — |
| **Nominated sub-contracts** | Principal selects, Contractor engages | Specialist quality control; arrangeable when full doc not ready | Contractor may have poor cause to object; bankruptcy → Principal pays for re-engagement |
| **Separate contracts** | Principal engages specialist concurrently with Contractor | Specialist control | Contractor + specialist may be incompatible; coordination friction; extra-of-time/cost claims |

**Principle:** keep nominated and separate contracts to a **minimum**; prefer specialist work as Trade sections of the BOQ.

## §3.5 Liquidated Damages

**Purpose:** (a) incentive for Contractor to complete on time; (b) enables Client to recover pre-estimated damages from late completion. Limits the moneys recoverable regardless of actual cost — protects Contractor.

**Basis of assessment** — one or more of:
| Component | Notes |
|---|---|
| Holding charges | Rates, taxes, interest on borrowed capital ÷ 52 = weekly rate |
| Loss of revenue | Where loss of rent / income readily established (residential, commercial) |
| Cost of alternative facilities | Rental costs in existing accommodation pending completion |
| Additional supervision and admin | Clerk of Works, supervisors, telephones, sheds, etc. |
| Effects of delays on other concurrent contracts | Delay costs on other projects |

LDs apply even if no actual loss — but must be carefully calculated to avoid load-on by Tenderers.

## §3.6 Delay Claims — Preliminaries-based evaluation

### Fundamentals for control
(i) Comprehensive contract conditions; (ii) accurate documentation; (iii) control over variation instructions; (iv) explicit guidelines on extensions of time and prolongation claims; (v) efficient admin during construction; (vi) on-site records of delays and effects on Preliminaries.

### Evaluation principles
- Limit to **on-site costs priced in Preliminaries**, in excess of allowances already in contract sum and previously priced for varied work
- **Exclude on-site costs of variations** (these are in variation pricing)
- **Cost Adjustment** during extension period taken into account
- Avoid attracting CA increments for total Contract period
- Prefer agreement at "current" costs (or agreed CA basis) — settle in progress payments ASAP

### Preliminaries categories
| Category | Behavior |
|---|---|
| **Time** | Total sum affected by an increase in contract time |
| **Time/Fixed** | Running costs only affected; establishment + removal not adjustable |
| **Value** | Total sum affected by an increase in value of works |
| **Actual** | Proof required (no CA payable) |
| **Non-adjustable** | Not affected by time or value increases |

Contractor submits breakdown of Preliminaries with **fixed** + **running** components shown for each item.

### Contentious items to **resist**
- Loss of profit
- Off-site overheads
- Lower productivity
- Cost Adjustment applied to delay claims
- Cost of preparing delay claims
- Loss of interest
- Reduced cash flow

Reimbursement should ideally be **on-site costs only**.

## §3.7 Variations

### Prevention of variations (mitigation)
1. High doc accuracy; sufficient time for doc preparation
2. Good design ↔ construction team communication; team continuity
3. All Client requirements fulfilled and confirmed
4. Effective communication via regular meetings
5. Clear distinction between variation and site instruction (clarification)
6. Confirm work is true variation (not already covered)
7. Single-point authorisation through Construction Project Leader
8. Adequate provisional sum allowances

### Contract Contingency
Absorbs cost effect of variations. Increases at Client's request → additional funds. Excess → compensating savings variations needed.

### Overheads, profit and attendance
- **On-site overheads** (supervision, offices, services, roads): in Preliminaries, no adjustment for variations except where specific additional preliminary cost shown
- **Off-site overheads** (directors fees, head office, taxes, fees, advertising): % addition to rates
- Margin for off-site overheads + profit on variations: typically **10%** for Contractor's own employees
- For nominated sub-contract variations: rate tendered against Provisional Sum
- Adjustment of attendance for NSC variations: only for changes in scope of NSC work

### Pricing methods for variations
1. **Bill rates** — labour + materials + plant + off-site overheads + profit (all at tender date)
2. **Schedule rates** — same components; rates are deemed to include cost of all works under contract
3. **Pro rata rates** — based on rates for similar items in Bill or Schedule
4. **Rates agreed/determined** between Contractor and Superintendent
5. **Labour rates** — agreed before tender acceptance ideally
6. **Daywork** — cost-plus; no incentive to control time/cost. State percentage in contract docs OR direct Tenderers to nominate

### Daywork is **not** subject to Cost Adjustment.

### Tender date as base
All Bill/Schedule/pro rata rates are at tender date. **All omissions** based on such rates. Therefore most appropriate system: all variation recommendations at tender date; current costs adjusted to that base date.

## §3.8 Monitoring and Reporting — the FF1–FF12 forms

Reports keep the project team advised of:
- Progress
- Current and forecast financial position
- Corrective actions taken/necessary

### Three parts of the Progress and Cost Report

**Part 1 — Synopsis** (FF1): Summary of Total Cost (cost distribution at tender acceptance / current / forecast final), commentary on redistribution and rate of contingency expenditure.

**Part 2 — Progress**:
- (i) Contract dates (tender, acceptance, possession, contract period in working days, completion date, approved time extensions, anticipated completion)
- (ii) Construction program status with comments
- (iii) Time extensions claimed (including granted/disallowed/under consideration)
- Photographs
- Summary of contract site instructions involving cost

**Part 3 — Cost** (formats FF2–FF12):

| Format | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **FF1** | Summary of total cost (synopsis) |
| **FF2** | Detailed statement of cost distribution and payments recommended |
| **FF3** | Reconciliation of contingent and other provisions (current and forecast) |
| **FF4** | Statement of current/forecast total cost (one per Builder's Work + each NSC) |
| **FF5** | Contract variations and adjustments (one per Builder's Work + each NSC; grouped by Variations / Provisional Sum adjustments / Provisional Quantity adjustments) |
| **FF6** | Total commitment cash flow report |
| **FF7** | Contract cash flow diagram (graphical) |
| **FF8** | Contract cash flow report (table) |
| **FF9** | Contract cost adjustment report |
| **FF10** | Progress payment summary |
| **FF11** | Progress payment calculations |
| **FF12** | Reconciliation of contract sum (one-time, at start of monitoring) |

### Cost report flow
The FF1 figures consolidate detailed FF2 information. References show the source format and column (e.g. "From FF3/8/C", "To FF1/3/B"). Approved adjustments separated for CA into:
- (a) Tender rates
- (b) Fixed (current) rates

### Progress payments
Established by grouping BOQ items into work sections (or detailed break-up of tender sum). Reduce by retention, previous payments, liquidated damages.

## §3.9 Cost Adjustment

### Two options to limit Cost Adjustment
**Both** decouple CA from actual contractor performance — interim payments based on actual performance, but total CA is on agreed/theoretical:

| Option | Basis |
|---|---|
| **Agreed Cash Flow** | Based on Contractor's construction program / predicted cash flow |
| **Theoretical Cash Flow** | Standard Performance Curve: % contract time → % contract value at coordinates (0, 0), (25, 10), (75, 80), (100, 100) |

Effects of either:
- (a) More accurate prediction of total CA — eliminates dependency on Contractor's actual performance
- (b) Inducement to complete before stipulated date
- (c) Fair and reasonable CA payments
- (d) Discourages inefficient construction practices

Both: where extension of time granted, value of work completed held constant for the extension duration.

### Contract time — the K formula
For establishing realistic construction times during documentation:

$$T = K \times C^{B}$$

where:
- T = duration in working days from possession to practical completion
- C = final cost in $millions adjusted to Sept 1972 prices ($1M Sep 1972 ≈ $7.5M Jan 2000)
- K = constant for time-performance level (varies by project type)
- B = constant for time effect of project size = **0.30**

K values:
| Type | K | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Normal construction | 313 | Medium rise + complex low-rise |
| Expedited | 250 | Low-rise (≤3 storeys) + medium-rise w/ significant prefabricated |
| Maximum | 407 | High-rise + complex medium-rise |
| Extremely expedited (5% of projects) | 173 | Straightforward + high prefab |

The formula is a first indicator only; final assessment considers:
(a) cash flow restrictions; (b) essential occupancy time; (c) locality; (d) lead times; (e) repetition; (f) construction type; (g) labour market.

For phased/staged projects: use network or precedence diagrams instead.
