A reference for **mechanical and electrical engineers** that supplements the procedures elsewhere in the manual. The integrated approach requires that all members of the project team understand each other's responsibilities.

## Aim

Set a realistic budget figure during initial assessment, and adhere to it through design and construction until completion.

## Key idea — engineer's vs architect's approach

| Architect | Engineer |
|---|---|
| Standard materials and systems, fully detailed and measured | System design, manufactured equipment, performance criteria — restricting suitable alternatives is undesirable |

Cost-management procedures in this section acknowledge these different approaches.

## What this scheme requires

(a) Comprehensive and reliable **bank of historical data** for estimating, with relevant indices to adjust  
(b) Use of this data by the appropriate engineer to compile a realistic budget **before** any detailed engineering design has commenced  
(c) Cost management of the project at various design stages — applying historical data, estimating more closely as design progresses, comparing to budget, taking remedial action by considering widest range of alternative systems  
(d) Obtaining and analysing project cost from Tenderers/Contractors, inserting back into the data bank for future projects

## §4.2 Method of Measurement of Building Areas

The engineer mostly uses two areas:

| Area | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **GFA** Gross Floor Area | Sum of FECA + UCA | Made available by team |
| **FECA** Fully Enclosed Covered Area | Sum of all areas at all building floor levels (incl. basements except unexcavated, floored roof spaces, attics, garages, penthouses, enclosed porches, attached enclosed covered ways, equipment rooms, lift shafts, vertical ducts, staircases, enclosed spaces and usable areas), measured from inside face of exterior walls; ignores plinths/columns/piers | Appendix A Part 2 |
| **UCA** Unenclosed Covered Area | Sum of all areas — roofed balconies, open verandahs, porches, porticos, attached open covered ways, undercrofts, unenclosed access galleries, trafficable covered areas not totally enclosed | Appendix A Part 2 |
| **UFA** Usable Floor Area | FECA less Common Use Areas, Service Areas, Non-habitable Areas | Appendix A Part 2 |
| **Treated Area (TA)** | Measured by the appropriate engineer | Each engineer's own basis |

## §4.3 Elements

Element = a portion of the project fulfilling a particular **physical purpose** irrespective of construction or specification (e.g. External Walls, Air Conditioning, Communications).

### Element groups
| Group | Numbers |
|---|---|
| Preliminaries | 00 |
| For each building: Substructure | 01 |
| Superstructure | 02–05 |
| External Fabric | 06–08 |
| Internal Fabric | 09–11 |
| Finishes | 12–14 |
| Fittings | 15–16 |
| Services | 17–29 |
| Centralised Energy Systems (total project) | 30 |
| Alterations (existing buildings) | 31 |
| External (total project) — Site Works | 32–36 |
| External Services | 37–44 |
| External Alterations | 45 |
| Special Provisions (total project) | 46 |

### Project structure
- For each building, elements 01–29 attribute costs to that building
- Plant supplying thermal/site-generated electrical energy to multiple buildings → element **30 CE**
- Site works and external connections → 31–45
- Special Provisions (contract contingency, fees, allowances) → element **46 YY** — included in Gross Project Cost (GPC), excluded from Net Project Cost (NPC)

### What's in element costs
Element costs include supply, installation and all necessary work for satisfactory operation of the equipment. Costs cover all charges including freight, insurance, travelling time, profit. Where relevant, include testing, commissioning, working drawings, manuals, "as installed" drawings.

## §4.4 Sub-elements and sub-element divisions

| Level | What | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | Element | All elements |
| 2nd | Sub-element | All elements |
| 3rd | Sub-element division | Engineering elements |
| 4th | Further sub-divisions (trades, object assemblies) | Discretion of Client/Authority |

### Sub-elements — two types
(a) Clear sub-divisions to define **sub-functions** (e.g. Special Services → various kinds)  
(b) Clear sub-division with **specification headings** (e.g. Light & Power → sub-mains, distribution boards, lighting system, power outlets)

### Units of measurement
- For convenience, **GFA** is the unit for project summaries
- Each sub-element has a particular unit when greater detail required
- Sub-element divisions: usually use the parent sub-element unit; additional units recommended for greater detail
- Until research determines best unit, multiple units listed → store quantities in as many different units as possible

## §4.5 Collection, analysis and storage of cost data

### Data collection
- Price data from Tenderers and Contractors
- For all significant projects, include a **schedule of prices** in tender documents
- For broad info: prices to element level only (all-inclusive per element)
- For full cost management: sub-element form, possibly sub-element divisions
- Specification clause obligates schedule completion
- Successful Tenderer typically submits within 28 days of acceptance

### Specification format
Each coded element / sub-element / sub-element division **matches** a specification section, clause or identifiable part. Tenderers/Contractors put prices against specification parts and transfer directly to schedule. Specification reorganisation may be required; specification clauses don't have to be in element sequence.

### Data analysis
- Schedule from successful Tenderer is chosen for analysis
- Comparison of all Tenderers' prices with historical data → tender evaluation + comparative cost info
- Forms used: Project Summary and Elemental Analysis form; Amplified Analysis form (per element)
- **Adjusted Contract Sum (ACS)** = sum after substituting actual accepted tender prices for all provisional sums

### Storage
File together: Project Summary + Elemental Analysis form / Amplified Analysis form / original price schedule / completed AIQS Standard Cost Analysis forms.

## §4.6 Engineering Cost Index

There is **no specific cost index** for engineering services. Fluctuations in engineering services cost are included in the Building Cost Index (BCI) — see Vol 1 §5.3.

## §4.7 Cost budgeting and cost planning of engineering services

### The four stages (mirror Vol 1 §2.2)

| Stage | Basis | Concern |
|---|---|---|
| A | Brief | Setting realistic budget |
| B | Outline proposals | Isolating best means of satisfying brief |
| C | Sketch design | Confirming design is effective + costs within budget; establishing elemental cost targets |
| D | Documentation | Continuous cost checks; ensuring cost based on finished documentation within budget |

### Cost data covers three areas
(a) Engineering Works **internal** to the building (elements 17–29)  
(b) **Centralised Energy** systems (element 30)  
(c) Engineering Works **external** to the building (elements 37–44)

Each engineer is responsible for costing their discipline's elements. **Early agreement** by team needed for shared elements (e.g. FP Fire Protection).

Use the list of elements/sub-elements as a "shopping list" check.

### Initial Budget (Brief Stage)
Engineering Works Internal — historical data or established rates by:
- (a) Functional Unit Costs (e.g. $/bed for hospital ward, $/pupil for school)
- (b) Functional Area Costs (pre-established costs for identified functional spaces + project-specific items)
- (c) Cost based on any other data

Centralised Energy — allowance based on best available data.  
External Services — length × rate per service, or allowance.

### Final Budget (Sketch Design)
Engineering Works Internal — same techniques but:
- Functional Areas adjusted to checked against outline sketches
- **Treated Area (TA)** for each element/sub-element measured, with TA-based rates

Centralised Energy + External Services estimated as accurately as possible.

The Initial Budget structure carries through; design contingency reduces.

### Stage C — Sketch Design Cost Plan
Information assumed available:
- (i) Final dimensioned sketch plans, elevations, sections, with specification notes
- (ii) Site layout (contours, roads, paths, landscaping, services, covered ways, fencing)

Basic engineering systems determined; if functional area costs used, standards established (number of points, gross air flow, lighting levels, number of outlets).

Estimating methods:
- (a) **Treated Area Costs** — TA per element/sub-element × TA rates for chosen system
- (b) **Engineering Unit Costs** — engineering requirements known → unit rates × engineering units (e.g. $/point, $/kW, $/L/s)

Index to current dates and modify for other factors.

Cost plan components:
- Internal Services by element
- Centralised Energy
- External Services by element
- Special Provisions
- Each element may include design contingency + allowance for other factors

### Stage D — Cost Management During Documentation
Continuous process — measured take-off + historical data / current rates → quantities. Cost information at element level minimum, possibly sub-element. Design contingency reduces to zero as documentation finalises. Concludes with cost plan based on tender documents + reconciliations with Sketch Design Cost Plan.

### Pre-Tender Cost Plan
Cost plan based on documentation prepared to **correspond exactly with the format of the Schedule of Prices** so detailed comparison highlights errors, omissions, wrong combinations.

## Other factors affecting engineering services costs

Apart from time escalation (covered by indices):
| Factor | Notes |
|---|---|
| Market conditions | Tendering market price index — long-term in BCI; short-term affects contract price |
| Locality | Cost variation by location (provincial, remote area) |
| Climate | Conditions imposed by climate of project area |
| Building criteria | Orientation, shape, materials |
| Site | Conditions, access, levels, availability of services |
| Construction time | Prolonged, staged, "fast-track" |
| Existing buildings | Location of existing services, time/noise restrictions |
| Fixed price | If contract is fixed-price, data from CA contracts may need varying |
| Codes and specifications | Changes can significantly affect price |
| Currency exchange | Fluctuations |
| Unusual contract conditions | D&C, extended guarantees |

## §4.8 Cost Management During Construction

Procedures in Vol 1 §3 apply. Factors that can escalate cost during construction:
(a) Tender Spec/Conditions of Contract; (b) tender doc quality; (c) progress payments; (d) cost adjustment provisions; (e) variations + Client requests; (f) supervision; (g) site relations; (h) prolongation claims; (i) industrial relations; (j) liquidated damages; (k) Contractor bankruptcy/default; (l) final accounts.

When: working on a project's engineering services cost (mechanical, electrical, hydraulic, fire, transportation); estimating engineering services as part of an Initial or Final Budget; setting up a cost data bank; designing a tender Schedule of Prices to capture sub-element costs; analysing tendered prices vs cost plan; comparing services costs to historical functional unit / treated area rates; identifying which discipline owns shared elements (e.g. fire protection); setting cost targets for centralised energy; managing engineering-services cost during construction.
